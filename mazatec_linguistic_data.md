## 1. Language Identification

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Language family | Oto-Manguean > Popolocan |
| ISO 639-3 codes | mau (Huautla), maq (Chiquihuitlán), maz (Jalapa de Díaz), and others |
| WALS codes | mzh (Huautla), mzc (Chiquihuitlán) |
| Autonym (language) | **Énná** |
| Ethnic self-designation | **Ha shuta Enima** ("workers of the mountains, humble people of custom") |
| Exonym origin | Nahuatl *mazatl* ("deer") → "Mazatec / people of the deer" |
| Speaker population | ~130,000–255,000 (Oaxaca, Puebla, Veracruz) |
| Prestige variety | Huautla de Jiménez (ISO: mau; ~50,000 speakers) |
| Geographic area | Sierra Mazateca, northern Oaxaca; districts of Teotitlán and Tuxtepec (Oaxaca), Tepexi (Puebla), and Tezonapa (Veracruz) |
| Word order | VSO (Verb–Subject–Object) |
| Official status | National language of Mexico (Ley General de Derechos Lingüísticos de los Pueblos Indígenas, 2003) |

## 2. Phonological System

### 2.1 Tone System

Mazatec is a tonal language in which tone is lexically and grammatically contrastive. The number of level tones varies by variety.

| Variety | Level tones | Contour tones noted |
|---|---|---|
| Huautla (mau) | 4 (high, semi-high, semi-low, low) | Yes |
| Jalapa de Díaz (maz) | 3 (high ˥, mid ˧, low ˩) | ˩˧, ˧˥, ˥˧, ˧˩, ˥˩, ˩˥˩ |
| Chiquihuitlán (maq) | 4 (high, midhigh, midlow, low) | 13 contour tones |
| San Jerónimo Tecóatl | 4 (high, semi-high, semi-low, low) | Yes |

#### Tonal notation conventions

| System | High | Semi-high | Mid | Semi-low | Low |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Diacritic | acute accent (á) | unmarked in some systems | macron (ā) or unmarked | subscript bar (a̠) | grave accent (à) or underline (a̱) |
| Numerical (Pike & Cowan 1957) | 1 | — | — | — | 4 |

#### Minimal tonal triplet (Jalapa Mazatec)

| Form | Tone | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| /ʃá/ | High | work |
| /ʃā/ | Mid | puma |
| /ʃà/ | Low | mould |

#### INALI tonal examples (multiple varieties)

| Mazatec form | Tone | Spanish gloss | English gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| xá | high | trabajo | work |
| xa | semi-high/mid | tigre / puma | puma |
| xa̠ | mid | borroso | blurry |
| jé | low | pecado | sin |
| jè | semi-low | él | he |
| je | low plain | gordo | fat |
| je̠ / je̠'e̠ | lowest | jilote | young corn ear |

### 2.2 Vowels

| Feature | Huautla (mau) | Jalapa (maz) |
|---|---|---|
| Vowel qualities | /i e a o/ (4) | /i æ a o u/ (5) |
| Nasal vowels | Yes | Yes |
| Modal voice | Yes | Yes |
| Breathy voice | Attested | Distinctive (unique to Jalapa) |
| Creaky voice | Yes | Yes |

Nasal vowels in writing: ⟨an, en, in, on, un⟩ (e.g., *én* = 'word, language').

### 2.3 Consonants

Mazatec consonant inventories include the following phonation types:

| Type | Description | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Prenasalized stops | Voiced stops with nasal onset | mb, nd |
| Aspirated | Aspiration following consonant | jt, tj, jk, kj |
| Glottalized | Glottal constriction | ʼb, ʼn |
| Pre-aspirated | h before stop | hk, ht |
| Pre-glottalized | glottal stop before consonant | ʼd, ʼg |
| Nasal clusters | Nasal + stop clusters | nts, nchr |
| Affricates | ts, tʃ, tʂ | ts, tch, tr |

Special regional consonants (Puebla/NW varieties): d, g, ds, dx

## 3. Writing System / Orthography

### 3.1 INALI Standard Alphabet (Énná/Mazatec, 2022)

The alphabet was developed through a 10-year community-based process (2012–2022) coordinated by INALI, covering all major Mazatec varieties.

**Vowels:** a, e, i, o, u
**Nasal vowels (written with following n):** an, en, in, on, un
**Tonal diacritics:** applied to vowels (see §2.1)
**Special phonation marks:** subscript bar (a̠) for certain tones/phonations

## 5. Morphology and Grammar Notes

### 5.1 Verbal Morphology

Mazatec verbs carry tonal and prefixal inflection. Present-tense verb prefixes attested in the literature:

| Prefix | Function |
|---|---|
| ki- | present / past |
| ti- | present |
| tsu- | present |
| tsi- | present |

Future tense: suffix **-ra** appended to verbal base.

### 5.2 Intransitive Verb Classes (San Jerónimo Tecóatl)

**Active subject verbs (selected):**

| Mazatec | Spanish | English |
|---|---|---|
| joó | platicar | to converse |
| tè | bailar | to dance |
| fì | ir, caminar | to go, walk |

**Non-active subject verbs (selected):**

| Mazatec | Spanish | English |
|---|---|---|
| tì | arder | to burn |
| tsʼiìn | nacer | to be born |
| kaà | caer | to fall |

### 5.3 Syntax

- Word order: **VSO** (Verb–Subject–Object)
- Relative pronoun **xi** is used before adjectives (obligatory when ≥2 adjectives present)
- Prenominal relative clauses

## 6. Historical Vocabulary Sources (19th–Early 20th Century)

The following three historical vocabulary lists appear as an appendix in Vielma Hernández (2017), *Panorama de los estudios lingüísticos sobre el mazateco*, and represent the primary data of the period relevant to the analyzed thesis document.

### 6.1 Brinton / Pinart 1892

**Source:** Brinton, Daniel G. 1892. "On the Mazatec Language of Mexico and Its Affinities." *Proceedings of the American Philosophical Society* 30(137):31–39.

**Data provenance:** Vocabulary collected by Alphonse Pinart, originally gathered by a Danish officer in service to Emperor Maximilian of Mexico (c. 1864–1867). Brinton compared Mazatec with Chiapanec-Mangue and Chibcha in this paper (a classification later rejected; Mazatec is Oto-Manguean).

*(Full word list entries from this 1892 vocabulary are contained in the appendix of Vielma Hernández 2017, Cuadernos de Lingüística de El Colegio de México 4(1):211–272; the PDF of the thesis was inaccessible during data collection.)*

### 6.2 Starr 1900

**Source:** Starr, Frederick. 1900. *Notes upon the Ethnography of Southern Mexico, Part 1.* Comparative vocabulary including: Chinantec, Chocho, Chontal, Huave, **Mazatec**, Mixe, Tepehua, Totonac, Triqui.

*(Full Mazatec word list contained in the same appendix as above.)*

### 6.3 Belmar 1905

**Source:** Belmar, Francisco. 1905b. *Lenguas indígenas de México.* Contains monosyllabic word examples for: Amuzgo, Cuicateco, Chatino, Chinantec, Chocho, **Mazatec**, Mixtec, Otomí, Popoloca, Triqui, Zapotec.

*(Full Mazatec examples contained in the same appendix as above.)*

## 7. Spanish Loanwords in Mazatec (Contact Phenomena)

The thesis (Vielma Hernández 2017) and the related Valle Alarcón (2016) study document how Spanish words are borrowed into Mazatec varieties. Key adaptation processes:

| Phonological process | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Initial segment deletion | Elision of word-initial consonant from Spanish | — |
| Final consonant deletion | Elision of final consonant cluster | — |
| Flap deletion | Loss of simple flap /ɾ/ in coda position | — |
| Vowel substitution | /o/ → /u/ | — |
| Sibilant realization | /s/ or /x/ realized as /ʃ/ | Spanish *sh*-like realization |
| Morpheme-based adaptation | Spanish verbs adapted via Mazatec morphemes (*ma*, *tsʼén*) | — |
| Tonal integration | Borrowed words assigned Mazatec tonal patterns | — |
| Glottal features | Aspirated, pre-aspirated, glottalized consonants added | — |

Pike & Cowan (1957) *Vocabulario mazateco* (Huautla): ~2,250 entries with tonal notation (superscript numerals 1 = high, 4 = low). This is the most extensive lexical compilation of Huautla Mazatec.
